# Design: HubSpot Signal-First Account Workspace

Status: DRAFT
Mode: Startup

## Problem Statement
Expansion-focused account managers and BDRs are forced to assemble account understanding across HubSpot activity history, Breeze research, LinkedIn Sales Navigator, ChatGPT/Perplexity, account-planning templates, and relationship-mapping tools. The data exists, but it is fragmented, query-driven, and hard to turn into action. The missing product is a signal-first, seller-readable workspace inside HubSpot that compresses research into commercial judgment.

## Demand Evidence
Current evidence is internal and directional, not yet fully validated.

Observed evidence from this session:
- The team already performs account research and planning work manually.
- Existing tools are used today: HubSpot CRM history, Breeze/company research agent, LinkedIn/Sales Navigator, ChatGPT/Perplexity, planning templates, and relationship tools like Swarm.
- The user identified a concrete failure mode: account managers look bad when accounts are happy and delivery is strong, but expansion still does not happen because signals, stakeholders, and next moves were not obvious.
- The user identified the expected value clearly: sellers should be able to "track signals very fast, at a glance understand what's happening with that company, and they don't need to do research. They know what the next actions we need to take are."

Evidence gap:
- No quantified proof yet on time saved per account, adoption willingness, or the number of accounts where this problem is painful enough to justify weekly use.

## Status Quo
Current workflow for new business and expansion:
1. Start in HubSpot and inspect prior emails, calls, meetings, and relationship history.
2. Use Breeze or HubSpot company research agent to ask ad hoc questions.
3. Jump to LinkedIn and Sales Navigator for account news, people research, account IQ, and manual org/relationship mapping.
4. Run external research in ChatGPT or Perplexity using prompts.
5. Pull findings into an account-planning template with sections like business overview, initiatives, stakeholders, KPIs, risks, and strategy.
6. Convert the research into outreach, social selling, intros, or account prioritization.

What breaks:
- Too many tabs and tools.
- Breeze depends too much on asking the right question.
- Signals are not tracked in one place.
- Org chart / relationship mapping is possible, but too manual to maintain.
- Presentation is heavy and not easy to skim.
- Sellers do the research ritual repeatedly instead of getting a fast read in HubSpot.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge
Primary user:
- Expansion-focused account manager working existing accounts.

What gets them promoted:
- Growing recurring revenue from existing accounts.
- Turning strong delivery and good relationships into additional commercial scope.

What makes them look bad:
- An account is healthy, the work is going well, and they still fail to expand it.
- They miss obvious openings because they did not see the signals, the stakeholders, or the right next move.

Narrowest wedge worth shipping first:
- A company-level signal-first research workspace inside HubSpot that gives the seller an immediate read on the account without forcing them through multiple tools.
- Chosen first-screen blocks:
  1. Signals
  2. People and reasons to talk
  3. Key initiatives
  4. Business overview

## Constraints
- Must live inside HubSpot as a company-record experience.
- Needs to work for expansion first, but not trap the product so tightly that new business becomes awkward later.
- Must prioritize speed to relevance over completeness.
- Must avoid becoming a bloated account-planning template in UI form.
- Trust is everything, so source evidence and distinction between inferred vs known data must be clear.
- Existing HubSpot extension work already follows a context -> fetch -> render pattern and can likely be reused.

## Premises
1. The core problem is not lack of data, it is lack of a signal-first, seller-readable view inside HubSpot.
2. Version 1 should optimize for fast commercial understanding on a company record, not for a full account-planning system.
3. The first wedge should serve expansion account managers, but the page structure should still work for new business later.
4. Signals should be the anchor of the page, with business overview, key initiatives, and people-to-contact supporting that anchor.
5. Org chart / relationship mapping is important, but should probably not be the center of version 1 unless it is mostly auto-generated.

## Approaches Considered
### Approach A: Signal-First Research Card
A lightweight company-level UI extension that generates a clean commercial brief focused on Signals, People to Contact, Key Initiatives, and Business Overview. Mostly read-only and optimized for fast learning.

Pros:
- Fastest to ship
- Easiest to test with real sellers
- Reuses prior extension patterns cleanly

Cons:
- Risks feeling like a prettier research report
- Weak persistence and collaboration
- Relationship intelligence remains shallow

### Approach B: Living Account Workspace
A CRM-native account workspace inside the company record with Signals as the anchor and structured blocks for People, Initiatives, overview, and recommended next moves. More than research, but not yet a full enterprise planning suite.

Pros:
- Better product value than a simple research page
- More likely to become part of weekly workflow
- Bridges static research and actual account execution
- Supports expansion now and net-new later

Cons:
- More design choices up front
- Higher risk of overbuilding if discipline slips
- Requires a clear distinction between AI-generated content and seller-edited content

### Approach C: Auto-Brief + Relationship Layer
A signal-first brief combined with auto-generated buying-group and lightweight org/relationship mapping as the hero differentiator.

Pros:
- Strongest differentiation
- Directly attacks the manual org-chart problem
- Powerful demo value

Cons:
- Highest complexity
- High trust risk if the people map is wrong
- Easy to impress in demo and disappoint in daily use

## Recommended Approach
Choose **Approach B, Living Account Workspace**.

Reason:
It is the best balance between speed and actual workflow value. Approach A is probably too close to a better-formatted report. Approach C is tempting but brittle because relationship mapping quality is hard to get right early. Approach B keeps the product focused on signal-first commercial understanding while leaving room for richer execution features later.

Recommended first-screen hierarchy:
1. Signals
2. Why now
3. People and reasons to talk
4. Key initiatives
5. Business overview

Recommended interaction model:
- Company-level HubSpot workspace
- Strong signal summary at the top
- Clear “reason to talk now” guidance per relevant person
- Evidence trail available on demand, not cluttering the default view
- Recommended next moves as a lightweight V1 object, not a giant action-planning system
- No separate full-brief destination in V1. The workspace itself must carry the value.

Recommended next moves schema:
`action | why now | linked person | linked signal | confidence`

## V1 Scope
### Must-have in V1
1. **Signals block**
   - Show 3-5 ranked signals.
   - Signals must be grouped by category: CRM activity, stakeholder changes, company/news triggers, and inferred commercial timing.
   - Each signal must show source, freshness, and confidence.
2. **Why now block**
   - A short synthesis derived from the top signals.
   - Must explain why the account deserves attention now without inventing a fake score.
   - Can show motion label such as `Expansion`, `New business`, or `Mixed` if the reasoning is visible.
3. **People and reasons to talk**
   - Output schema: `first name | last name | title | location | CRM status | likely role | reason to talk now | evidence | suggested next action`.
   - Start with known HubSpot contacts and associated-company contacts first.
   - Show whether a person is already in HubSpot or is only inferred / externally found.
   - Inferred roles are allowed, but must be labeled as inferred.
4. **Key initiatives**
   - Show 2-4 likely active initiatives tied to evidence.
   - Each initiative must connect back to at least one signal or source.
5. **Business overview**
   - Keep this short and above-the-fold friendly.
   - Include what they do, who they sell to, and strategic context relevant to outreach.
6. **Source drawer / evidence view**
   - Seller can inspect the underlying sources without leaving the page.

### Explicit non-goals for V1
- No full editable account-planning template.
- No heavy org-chart builder or manual relationship-map canvas.
- No separate full-brief screen or fake drill-down destination.
- No large narrative report as the primary view.
- No requirement to support every account equally, V1 should focus on high-value accounts only.
- No opaque score that cannot be explained by visible evidence.

### Content ownership model
- V1 should be **mostly read-only**.
- AI/system-generated content is primary.
- Seller input should be limited to lightweight actions such as refresh, open sources, and possibly save a simple note later, but annotations are not required for first ship.

### Eligibility / targeting rule
A company should get the full workspace only if at least one of the following is true:
- it is a strategic or named account
- it has recent meaningful signal activity
- it already has a relationship history worth expanding

Everything else can stay on lighter monitoring.

### Canonical user flows
1. **Pre-call prep**
   - Seller opens company record before a meeting.
   - Workspace answers: what changed, who matters, what angle to use.
2. **Weekly account review**
   - Seller or manager reviews top accounts.
   - Workspace answers: which accounts are hot, what new signals appeared, where follow-up is justified.
3. **Re-entry / expansion scan**
   - Seller checks a previously successful account.
   - Workspace answers: is there a new initiative, role change, or buying signal that makes re-engagement timely.

### Data/source mapping
| Block | Primary sources | Known vs inferred | Freshness rule | Fallback |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Signals | HubSpot activity, company/contact records, external research, LinkedIn/Sales Nav style inputs | Mixed | show last updated timestamp | hide weak signals if evidence is stale |
| People and reasons to talk | HubSpot contacts, associated company contacts, enrichment, prior relationship history | Mixed | refresh on demand | show known contacts only |
| Key initiatives | external research, CRM notes, prior project context | Mostly inferred | regenerate when signals refresh | omit if weak evidence |
| Business overview | company profile, CRM, external research | Mixed | slower-changing | show simple firmographic summary |

### Trust and UX rules
- Every inferred claim needs visible source access.
- Every signal needs a timestamp.
- Confidence labels should be explicit: known, likely, inferred.
- If motion is labeled `Expansion`, `New business`, or `Mixed`, the reasons must be visible.
- If a person is not in HubSpot, the UI must say so clearly instead of implying CRM ownership.
- Empty and failure states matter. If enrichment fails, the user should still see first-party HubSpot context.
- Above the fold must answer the 15-second questions: what changed, why now, who matters, and whether this is mainly expansion or new business.
- Prefer an A4-style readable layout for the full brief and desktop-first workspace design. Mobile is not a V1 requirement.

### Platform stance
- Design for a HubSpot company-record workspace first.
- If architecture is not settled yet, choose the UX structure first but keep the content model compatible with either native blocks or a hybrid/iframe-backed view.
- Do not let architecture uncertainty expand scope.

## Open Questions
1. What exact first-party signals are available and reliable from HubSpot today?
2. Which third-party signals are permissible, affordable, and maintainable for this internal workflow?
3. Who owns editing or correcting inferred stakeholder roles?
4. Should sellers be able to annotate the workspace, or should v1 remain read-only plus refresh?
5. What should the scoring model be for account health / urgency, if any, in v1?
6. How many accounts per seller actually deserve the full workspace treatment versus lightweight monitoring only?

## Success Criteria
- An expansion-focused seller can open a company record and understand the account situation in under 15 seconds.
- Prep time for a targeted account review drops by at least 50% versus the current manual workflow.
- At least 80% of shown signals are judged useful or credible by internal users during pilot review.
- Sellers can identify at least one credible person to contact and one credible reason to contact them.
- At least 60% of pilot sessions result in a seller action such as opening a source, selecting a contact, or using the suggested next move in outreach prep.
- Pilot users return to the workspace on repeated account checks instead of restarting research from scratch.
- Internal users describe it as helping them know "what changed, why it matters, who matters, and what to do next."

## Dependencies
- Existing HubSpot UI extension scaffolding / extension deployment path
- Reliable company and contact context from HubSpot record pages
- Research/enrichment layer for signals and account summary
- Prompting and summarization logic adapted from existing research workflows
- Decision on whether v1 renders inline native UI, iframe-backed workspace, or a hybrid

## The Assignment
Take 5 real existing accounts and run the current manual process on each one. For each account, record:
- how long prep actually took
- which tabs/tools were used
- which signals mattered in hindsight
- who the seller would contact
- what they would say

If this is not painful and repetitive across 5 accounts, the product is weaker than it looks. If the pattern repeats, you have your proof.
